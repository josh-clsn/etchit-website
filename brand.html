This is the complete brand system for etchit — logo, name, color, typography, voice, components, and applications. It exists because permanent infrastructure deserves a permanent visual identity: one that reads the same in a terminal, on an app icon, and on the side of a t-shirt ten years from now. Everything in this document is canonical. If a file in the repo disagrees with what's here, the file is wrong.

01 / Principles

Five rules, one feeling.

Every design decision in this system answers to these five principles. When in doubt, pick the one closest to permanent and the one furthest from decorative.

01
Permanent by default.

Nothing we design should look like it expires. No trend-chasing gradients, no seasonal refreshes, no illustrations that read 2024-but-not-2030. If it can't still feel right a decade from now, it's wrong today.

02
Terminal, not tech.

We speak monospace, not marketing. Glyphs over icons, brackets over buttons, addresses over avatars. The interface should feel like something a developer left running — precise, confident, a little unfinished on purpose.

03
Editorial restraint.

One italic serif, used sparingly, does more than a dozen flourishes. Instrument Serif is the only non-mono voice in the system — and it only speaks to emphasize, never to decorate.

04
One color that matters.

Copper. Not orange, not amber, not rust — copper. It's the only chromatic color in the system. Everything else is ink, bone, or ash. If you need a second accent, you don't need a second accent.

05
The slash is the brand.

/ is not a stylistic choice, it's the identity. It appears in the wordmark, the mark, the favicon, the copy, the code. Treat it like a logomark: never tilt it, never color it anything but copper, never replace it with a substitute character.

// the system
Keep the slash. Drop it only when the parser will.

The slash isn't a problem to work around — it's the brand. Everything else follows from one question: does this surface let me style type?

  • etch/it — wherever you control the typography
  • etchit — wherever a parser, URL bar, or form will touch it
  • [/] — wherever there's no room for the full wordmark
  • "etch it" — whenever it's spoken, never written

The tagline "Etch it. Fetch it." is the secret weapon. Put it in the App Store subtitle, the meta description, and every intro — it teaches pronunciation without effort. After someone sees it once, etchit reads correctly forever.

06 / Voice

Short. Declarative. Slightly severe.

We write like a thing that doesn't need you. Not cold — confident. Permanent infrastructure shouldn't apologize, explain itself twice, or ask for your email.

✓ Do
It stays exactly as you etched it.
Direct. Declarative. Makes a promise without hedging.
Pay once. Stored forever.
Short. Two fragments do the work of a paragraph.
No accounts. No moderators. No delete button.
Lists of absences. Shows the product by showing what it doesn't have.
The link will still work in ten years.
Concrete. A decade is a vivid number; "forever" is an abstraction.
✕ Don't
Welcome to etchit — the revolutionary new way to store text!
"Revolutionary" and "new" both date us. Welcome-copy is saas energy.
We believe data should be permanent, so we built...
No first-person plural. The product speaks, not the company.
Please sign up to get started with your first etch!
There are no signups. Excited punctuation is begging.
Seamlessly store, share, and manage your permanent content.
"Seamlessly" and "manage" are both banned. We use verbs, not adverbs.
// vocabulary
The words we own. And the ones we don't say.
Lexicon

etch · to write permanently · fetch · to read by address · address · the 64-char handle · permanent · preferred over "immutable" in marketing · immutable · preferred over "permanent" in technical copy · the network · Autonomi, shortened after first mention · ANT · the token, always all-caps · etcetera · the catch-all use case · .

Banned

blockchain (say "the network") · crypto (say "ANT" or nothing) · web3 · platform (we're a primitive) · solution · seamlessly · revolutionary · user (say "you" or "anyone") · content (say "text" or "paste") · leverage · empower.

Note on decentralized: allowed. Use when it's the most accurate word for the architecture — especially in top-of-funnel copy where readers already have a mental model for it. Pair with concrete alternatives like public peer-to-peer network when describing internals.

// punctuation as design
Three marks do most of the work.
/
The brand mark. Separator in the wordmark, bullet in the mark, title-tag decoration.
·
Middle dot. Separates tagline halves, marketing specs ("no accounts · no servers · no delete button").
.
The period. Our single favorite mark. End fragments. Full stops. "Etch it. Fetch it." is half built out of it.
[/]
The mark in text. Use in eyebrows, bullet lists, ornamental dividers between paragraphs.

08 / Motion

Type in. Fade up. Never bounce.

Motion should feel mechanical — a thing being printed, not a thing being performed. No spring physics, no overshoots, no easing that smiles.

Terminal type-in
0.4s · ease-out · staggered 0.2–0.4s
Lines appear in sequence, each one slightly left-then-resting. Used in the hero terminal demo and anywhere showing a "just ran this" state.
Fade-up (scroll)
0.8s · ease-out · translateY(30 → 0)
Section reveal triggered by IntersectionObserver at ~15% visibility. One section at a time; no cascading.
Cursor blink
1.1s · step-end · on/off
A single copper block cursor following the wordmark. Step-end, not ease — terminals don't fade.
Pulse
2s · infinite · opacity + scale(1 → 1.3)
The status dot in the eyebrow. Slow, quiet — signal of life, not attention-grab.
Button press
0.2s · translate + offset-shadow
Primary buttons shift up-and-left with a hard copper-dim drop-shadow. Stamping, not floating.
Marquee
40s · linear · infinite · translateX(0 → −50%)
Horizontal band of all-caps claims. Slow enough to read, not so slow it feels broken.
Item hover
0.3s · ease · padding-left + background
List items (uses, specs) shift right and darken. Indicates the item is actionable without introducing a button.

Rule of thumb: if you'd describe the motion with a verb like "pops", "bounces", "springs", or "wobbles", it's wrong for this brand. Correct verbs: prints, stamps, reveals, slides, fades, blinks.
